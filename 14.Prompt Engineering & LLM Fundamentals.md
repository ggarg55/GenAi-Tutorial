# Prompt Engineering & LLM Fundamentals

## A Complete Guide to Crafting Effective Prompts and Controlling Model Behavior

---

### Introduction

Large Language Models (LLMs) have transformed how we interact with AI. But getting the best results isn’t just about typing a question—it’s about *engineering* the input to guide the model toward the output you need. This tutorial covers everything from choosing the right model to fine‑tuning its creativity, and from basic prompt structure to advanced techniques like chain‑of‑thought and automated prompt generation.

By the end, you’ll have a practical toolkit to consistently produce high‑quality outputs from any LLM.

---

## Part 1: Choosing the Right Model

Before you write your first prompt, you need to decide *which* model to use. The choice boils down to two fundamental questions:

1. **Proprietary vs. open‑source?**  
2. **How much control do you need over the output?**

### Proprietary Models

Think of these as *renting a luxury car with a chauffeur*.  
Examples: GPT‑4, Claude, Gemini.

| Pros | Cons |
|------|------|
| Polished, high performance out of the box | Pay per use (API costs) |
| No infrastructure setup | Limited customization |
| Great for production applications | You don’t own the model |

### Open‑Source Models

Think of these as *buying a project car*.  
Examples: LLaMA, Mistral, Falcon.

| Pros | Cons |
|------|------|
| Free to use on your own hardware | Requires technical setup |
| Full flexibility – fine‑tune, modify, run locally | Varies widely in size and quality |
| Available in multiple sizes (e.g., 7B, 13B, 70B parameters) | May need more prompt tuning |

**Recommendation:** Start with a smaller open‑source model to prototype. Once you’ve validated your approach, scale up to larger models or proprietary APIs. It’s far easier to go from small to large than the reverse.

## Part 2: Controlling Model Outputs – Temperature & Top_p

Once you’ve picked a model, you can control its “creativity” using two key parameters: **Temperature** and **Top_p** (also called nucleus sampling). These act like dials that shape how random or predictable the output will be.

### The “I’m driving a…” Example

Consider the prompt:  
`I'm driving a`

The model internally assigns probabilities to the next word, based on its training. A simplified distribution might look like:

| Word     | Probability |
|----------|-------------|
| car      | 40%         |
| vehicle  | 30%         |
| bike     | 15%         |
| truck    | 10%         |
| cycle    |  5%         |
| meeting  |  0.5%       |

### Temperature

**What it does:** Controls *randomness* – how likely the model is to pick less‑probable words.

- **T = 0** → Always picks the most likely word (“car” every time).  
  *Deterministic, predictable output.*

- **T = 1** → Samples according to the original distribution.  
  *Normal randomness.*

- **T > 1** → Makes the distribution more uniform, increasing the chance of rare words (“meeting”).  
  *High randomness, creative but may become nonsensical.*

**Mental model:** A dimmer switch for creativity. Low = safe, high = surprising.

### Top_p (Nucleus Sampling)

**What it does:** Controls *which subset of tokens* the model can choose from, by cumulative probability.

- Set `top_p = 0.4`.  
  Start with the highest probability words and add them until their cumulative probability reaches 0.4.

  ```
  car (0.4) → cumulative = 0.4 → STOP
  ```
  Only “car” is eligible.

- Set `top_p = 0.7`.  
  ```
  car (0.4) → cumulative = 0.4
  vehicle (0.3) → cumulative = 0.7 → STOP
  ```
  Both “car” and “vehicle” are eligible.

**Mental model:** A “vocabulary pool” size. Smaller `top_p` = more focused output.

### Combining Temperature & Top_p for Different Tasks

| Task                | Temperature | Top_p | Why? |
|---------------------|-------------|-------|------|
| **Email generation** | Low         | Low   | Predictable, professional, no random words. |
| **Translation**      | Low         | High  | Deterministic meaning, but flexible vocabulary choices. |
| **Creative writing** | High        | Low   | High creativity within a controlled word pool to maintain coherence. |
| **Brainstorming**    | High        | High  | Maximum diversity to generate novel ideas. |

> **Important note:** These settings interact. For example, high temperature combined with very low `top_p` can cause *hallucinations* because the model is forced to pick from a tiny pool of tokens while also being encouraged to pick unlikely ones. Always test combinations on your specific use case.

## Part 3: The Anatomy of a Prompt

A prompt is more than just a question. It’s a structured message that tells the model what to do and how to respond.

### Basic Prompt Components

1. **Instruction** – What you want the model to do.  
2. **Data** – The information to process.  
3. **Output indicator** – How the response should be formatted.

**Example:**
```
Instruction: Classify the text as positive or negative.
Data: "I won the match!"
Output indicator: Sentiment:
```
*Output:* `Positive` (clean, without extra text like “The sentiment is…”)

### Advanced Prompt Ingredients

You can add more elements to shape the response:

| Ingredient | Example |
|------------|---------|
| **Persona** | “You are an expert in foundation models…” |
| **Context** | “I’m writing this email to a hiring manager…” |
| **Format** | “Return your answer as a bulleted list.” |
| **Audience** | “Explain like I’m 5 (ELI5).” |
| **Tone** | “Use a professional, formal tone.” |

A prompt that includes these elements is much more likely to produce a useful, tailored output.

## Part 4: Prompt Types & Techniques

### Zero‑Shot Prompting

No examples are provided; just the instruction.

```
Classify the text as positive or negative.
Text: "I fell from my cycle."
Sentiment:
```

### One‑Shot Prompting

You give a single example to illustrate the task.

```
Classify the text as positive or negative.

Text: "I won the match!" → Sentiment: Positive

Text: "I fell from my cycle." → Sentiment:
```

### Few‑Shot Prompting

Multiple examples are provided. This works well for complex tasks where the format or nuance matters.

### Chain‑of‑Thought (or Chain Prompts)

Instead of asking for everything in one go, you break the task into a sequence of steps. Each step’s output feeds into the next.

**Example: Building a marketing campaign**

1. **Product name**  
   `Create a product name based on these features.`  
   → Output: `"EcoCharge"`

2. **Slogan**  
   `Create a slogan using the name "EcoCharge".`  
   → Output: `"Power your day, save the planet."`

3. **Sales pitch**  
   `Write a sales pitch using the name "EcoCharge" and the slogan "Power your day, save the planet."`  
   → Output: a complete marketing page.

**Why this works:** The model focuses on one subtask at a time, often producing higher‑quality results than a single complex prompt.

## Part 5: Advanced Topic – Using LLMs to Generate Prompts

A modern trend is to let the model itself create the prompt. This can save time and improve quality, but it comes with trade‑offs.

### Benefits

- **Speed & Scaling** – Generate hundreds of prompt variations quickly.  
- **Consistency** – Maintain the same tone and structure across prompts.  
- **Lower effort for non‑experts** – Anyone can produce well‑structured prompts.  
- **Idea expansion** – Turn vague ideas into detailed, effective prompts.

### Risks & Pitfalls

- **Loss of intent clarity** – The model may misinterpret what you really need.  
- **Feedback loop problems** – If you use AI to generate prompts and then feed those prompts back into an AI without human review, outputs can become repetitive or narrow.  
- **Hidden biases** – Training‑data biases can be baked into the prompts.  
- **Over‑optimization** – The prompt may look perfect but fail in real‑world scenarios.

**Best practice:** Use LLMs to generate prompt *candidates*, but have a domain expert review and refine the final prompts. Human oversight remains essential.

---

## Summary: Key Takeaways

- **Model choice** = Proprietary (high performance) vs. Open‑source (flexibility, lower cost).  
- **Temperature** controls randomness: low = predictable, high = creative.  
- **Top_p** controls vocabulary range: low = focused, high = diverse.  
- A **well‑crafted prompt** includes Instruction + Data + Output Indicator, and optionally Persona, Context, Format, Audience, Tone.  
- **Prompt types** progress from zero‑shot → one‑shot → few‑shot → chain‑of‑thought.  
- **Automated prompt generation** is powerful but needs human oversight to avoid bias and feedback loops.

## Practice Exercise

Try this exercise to see these concepts in action. Use any LLM you have access to.

1. **Simple prompt**  
   `Write a job rejection email.`  
   Run it with `temperature = 0`, `top_p = 0.1`.  
   Run it with `temperature = 0.8`, `top_p = 0.9`.  
   Compare the outputs.

2. **Add persona**  
   `You are a compassionate HR professional. Write a job rejection email.`

3. **Add tone**  
   `You are a compassionate HR professional. Use a warm but professional tone. Write a job rejection email.`

4. **Chain‑of‑thought**  
   - First, generate a list of reasons to give in the rejection.  
   - Then, write the email incorporating those reasons.

Notice how each addition changes the quality, style, and appropriateness of the output.
