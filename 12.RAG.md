# From Search to RAG: A Practical Guide to Retrieval Augmented Generation

In this tutorial, we’ll explore how modern AI systems combine search and language models to produce factual, grounded answers. We’ll start with the building blocks of intelligent search and gradually build up to Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG)—a technique that powers many question‑answering systems, chatbots, and AI assistants today.

By the end, you’ll understand:

- How **dense retrieval** finds documents by meaning, not just keywords.
- Why **reranking** improves the quality of search results.
- What **RAG** is and how it reduces hallucinations.
- Advanced RAG patterns like **query rewriting**, **multi‑query**, and **multi‑hop**.
- How to evaluate a RAG system using metrics like citation recall and faithfulness.
- The current **limitations** of RAG and how they are being addressed.

We’ll use a running example: a **Cooking Assistant** that answers questions based on a collection of recipes. This will make every concept concrete and easy to remember.

## 1. The Foundation: How Do We Find Information?

Before we can augment generation, we need to understand how a machine finds relevant information. There are two main approaches: lexical search and dense retrieval.

### 1.1 Lexical Search – The Classic Way
Think of the `Ctrl+F` (or `Cmd+F`) function in your browser. You type a word, and the system finds every page containing that exact word.  
**Example:** You search for `"chicken"` and get all recipes that mention “chicken”.  
**Limitation:** If you search for `"poultry"`, you’ll miss every chicken recipe because the exact word “poultry” isn’t there. Lexical search doesn’t understand meaning.

### 1.2 Dense Retrieval – Searching by Meaning
Dense retrieval uses **embeddings**—numerical representations of text that capture semantic meaning. Words or sentences with similar meanings are placed close together in a high‑dimensional space.

**How it works:**
1.  Every document (recipe) is converted into an embedding.
2.  Your question is also converted into an embedding.
3.  The system calculates the similarity (e.g., cosine similarity) between the question embedding and all document embeddings.
4.  It returns the documents with the highest similarity scores.

**Example:** You ask *“How do I cook poultry?”*  
The embedding for “poultry” is mathematically close to the embedding for “chicken”, so the system retrieves chicken recipes—even though the exact word “chicken” wasn’t in your query.

This is the essence of **semantic search**—finding results based on meaning rather than keyword matching.

---

## 2. Refining Results with Reranking

Even the best dense retrieval can return a broad list of candidates. **Reranking** is a second, more precise step that reorders the retrieved documents to put the most relevant ones at the top.

Think of it as a two‑stage process:

1. **Retrieval (broad)** – A fast model fetches the top‑K (say 20) most relevant documents.
2. **Reranking (precise)** – A slower but more powerful model re‑scores these 20 documents against the query and outputs a final, highly relevant order (e.g., the top 5).

**Why it matters:** The initial retrieval might include documents that are *semantically* close but not the perfect match. Reranking fixes that by applying a more nuanced understanding.

---

## 3. Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG)

Now we come to the star of the tutorial: **RAG**. It combines retrieval with a Large Language Model (LLM) to generate answers that are factual, up‑to‑date, and backed by sources.

### 3.1 The Problem with Pure LLMs
A typical LLM (like ChatGPT) knows only what it was trained on. If you ask a question about a recent event or a private document, it will either:

- Give an incorrect answer (a **hallucination**), or
- Say it doesn’t know.

### 3.2 The RAG Pattern
RAG solves this by giving the LLM a “cheat sheet” of relevant information before it answers.

1. **Query** – The user asks a question.  
   *Example: “What’s a good chicken recipe for a beginner?”*

2. **Retrieval** – The system searches a knowledge base (your recipe collection) using dense retrieval and reranking to get the most relevant documents.

3. **Augmentation** – The original query is **augmented** with the retrieved content. A new prompt is built:  
   *“Using the following recipes as reference, answer the question: ‘What’s a good chicken recipe for a beginner?’  
   Recipe 1: […]  
   Recipe 2: […]  
   Recipe 3: […]”*

4. **Generation** – The LLM generates an answer, but now it is **grounded** in the provided context. It can also include citations (e.g., “Source: Recipe Book, page 42”).

**Benefits:**
- **Reduces hallucinations** – The LLM is summarising provided text, not inventing facts.
- **Increases factuality** – You can verify answers by checking the sources.
- **Application‑specific** – You can build a Q&A system for your own documents (e.g., company manuals, legal contracts, research papers).

---

## 4. Advanced RAG Techniques

Basic RAG works well, but real‑world questions can be tricky. The following techniques make RAG smarter.

### 4.1 Query Rewriting
Users often ask vague or complex questions. A **query rewriter** (another LLM) simplifies them into search‑friendly queries.

**Example:**  
User: *“I need to make something for guests who are vegetarian and don’t like mushrooms.”*  
Rewritten query: *“Vegetarian recipes, no mushrooms.”*

The rewritten query leads to better retrieval results.

### 4.2 Multi‑Query RAG
Sometimes a single question contains multiple parts that should be searched separately.

**Example:** *“Compare the cooking time of roasted chicken in the 2025 and 2026 editions.”*  
The system splits this into two queries:
- *“Roasted chicken cooking time, 2025 edition”*
- *“Roasted chicken cooking time, 2026 edition”*

It retrieves documents for each, then the generation model combines the information into a coherent comparison.

### 4.3 Multi‑Hop RAG
In a multi‑hop question, answering the second part depends on the answer to the first part.

**Example:** *“Who won the ‘Best Vegetarian Chef’ award in 2023, and what is their signature dish?”*

- **Hop 1:** Retrieve documents about the 2023 award → find “Chef Anna”.
- **Hop 2:** Use “Chef Anna” to retrieve her signature dish.
- **Hop 3:** Generate a final answer that includes both pieces.

Multi‑hop RAG chains retrievals together, mimicking human reasoning.

### 4.4 Query Routing
If your knowledge base is split across multiple data sources, you can route each query to the most appropriate source.

**Example:** Your Cooking Assistant has two databases:
- “Quick Recipes” (for fast meals)
- “Professional Techniques” (for advanced skills)

- A query like *“What’s a 15‑minute pasta?”* routes to **Quick Recipes**.
- A query like *“How do I temper chocolate?”* routes to **Professional Techniques**.

Routing makes the system more efficient and accurate.

## 5. Evaluating a RAG System

Building a RAG system is only half the battle. You must measure its quality. Here are the key metrics used in practice:

| Metric | What it measures |
|--------|------------------|
| **Fluency** | Is the generated text natural, grammatical, and cohesive? |
| **Perceived Utility** | Is the answer actually helpful? (A fluent but useless answer is still bad.) |
| **Citation Recall** | Does every claim have a supporting citation? |
| **Citation Precision** | Do the citations actually support the claims? (No “fake” references.) |
| **Faithfulness / Consistency** | Does the answer stay true to the retrieved context? |
| **Answer Relevance** | Is the answer on‑topic? (Not a tangent.) |

**Pro tip:** You can use another LLM as a judge to automatically score these metrics, but human evaluation is still the gold standard for critical applications.

## 6. Limitations and the Road Ahead

No technology is perfect. A common limitation of RAG arises when dealing with very large, heterogeneous datasets:

> *When you have a vast dataset with very different kinds of information, vector search (dense retrieval) can struggle. In such cases, traditional lexical search (like BM25) sometimes performs better.*

**Why this happens:** Dense retrieval relies on embedding similarity, which can sometimes pull documents that are *semantically* related but *factually* irrelevant for the specific question.

**How the field is responding:**
- **Hybrid search** – Combining lexical search (BM25) and dense retrieval, then reranking the results.
- **Hierarchical indexing** – First find the right *chapter* or *section*, then search within it.
- **Fine‑tuned retrievers** – Training the embedding model specifically on the domain of interest (e.g., legal documents, medical texts).

There is no single “right answer”—the best approach depends on your data and use case. Experimentation is key.

## 7. Putting It All Together: The Cooking Assistant

Let’s walk through a complete example that ties together every concept.

1. **User query:** *“Show me a chicken recipe that’s quick and uses ingredients I probably have at home.”*

2. **Query rewriting:**  
   *“Quick chicken recipe, common ingredients.”*

3. **Retrieval:** Dense retrieval fetches 20 relevant recipes from the recipe database. Reranking reorders them, bringing the top 5 to the front.

4. **Multi‑query (if needed):** If the system also had a “tips” database, it might simultaneously retrieve *“how to cook chicken quickly”* from that source.

5. **Augmentation:** The final prompt includes the top recipes and perhaps the tips.

6. **Generation:** The LLM produces an answer:
   > *“One great option is ‘15‑Minute Garlic Butter Chicken’ from the Quick Recipes collection. It uses chicken breast, garlic, butter, and paprika—ingredients you likely have. (Source: Quick Recipes, p. 23)”*

7. **Evaluation:** A reviewer checks that the answer is fluent, useful, consistent with the recipe, and that the citation is correct.
