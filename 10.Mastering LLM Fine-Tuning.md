# Mastering LLM Fine-Tuning: From Pre-training to Parameter‑Efficient Methods

- Practical insights on data size, RAG vs. fine‑tuning, and when to use each approach

Let’s dive in.

## 1. Transfer Learning: The Big Idea

Imagine you want to become a world‑class chef. You first learn universal skills: how to hold a knife, boil water, season food. Later you specialise in Italian cuisine.  
This is **transfer learning**: you take general knowledge and adapt it to a specific domain.

For LLMs, transfer learning consists of:

1. **Pre‑training** – learning language from massive, unlabelled data (like reading thousands of cookbooks).
2. **Fine‑tuning** – specialising the model on your own task (like focusing on Italian recipes).

> **Transfer learning = Pre‑training + Fine‑tuning**

---

## 2. Pre‑training: Building the Foundation

A foundation model (e.g., GPT, Llama, BERT) is created by pre‑training on huge amounts of text—often trillions of words from the internet, books, and articles.  
During pre‑training, the model learns to **predict the next word** (or token). This is called **language modeling**.

**Example:**  
Given the sentence “The cat sat on the ___”, the model learns to predict “mat”.  
It does this without any labelled data; the task itself creates the labels (the next word).

The result is a **base model** that understands grammar, facts, reasoning patterns, and context.  
But this model doesn’t know how to answer questions or follow instructions—it only knows how to complete text.

---

## 3. Supervised Fine‑Tuning (SFT): Teaching Instructions

Suppose you ask a base model:  
> “What is your name?”

Because it was trained to complete sentences, it might reply:  
> “What is your name and what is your address?”

It’s extending the sentence instead of answering.  
**Supervised fine‑tuning** fixes this by training the model on **instruction‑response pairs**.

### How SFT works
You collect (or create) a dataset where each example has an instruction and the desired response.

| Instruction | Desired Response |
|-------------|------------------|
| What is 2 + 3? | 5 |
| Write a short poem about autumn. | (a short poem) |
| Explain transfer learning. | (a concise explanation) |

After SFT, the model learns to *follow instructions* rather than just complete them.  
Now when you ask “What is 2 + 3?”, it correctly answers “5”.

> **SFT = Instruction Tuning**

---

## 4. Full Fine‑Tuning (FFT): Domain Specialisation

Sometimes you need a model that is an expert in a specific domain—say, medical diagnosis, legal documents, or local history.  
**Full fine‑tuning** takes the pre‑trained (or SFT‑ed) model and continues training on your **labelled domain data**.

### Example
You want a model that excels at answering questions about the historical places of Jodhpur.  
You gather 500 question‑answer pairs about Jodhpur and fine‑tune the model on them.  
Now the model not only knows how to answer questions (thanks to SFT) but also possesses deep knowledge about Jodhpur.

**Key difference:**
- **SFT** teaches the model *how to answer* (instruction following).  
- **FFT** teaches the model *what to answer* (domain expertise).

---

## 5. Preference Tuning: Aligning with Human Taste

After SFT, you can further refine the model to match **human preferences**.  
For example, you might prefer short, bullet‑point answers, or answers with a friendly tone.  
Preference tuning uses data where humans rank responses, teaching the model to produce the preferred style.

> **Preference tuning** = “Answer in a way users like.”

---

## 6. Full Fine‑Tuning Is Expensive – Enter PEFT

Full fine‑tuning is powerful but comes with heavy costs:

- **Compute** – training all parameters requires many GPUs
- **Time** – slow to train
- **Storage** – you must store a separate copy of the full model for each task

**Parameter‑Efficient Fine‑Tuning (PEFT)** solves this by training only a small fraction of the parameters, while keeping the rest frozen.  
The most common PEFT methods are **adapters** and **LoRA**.

---

## 7. Adapters: The Modular Approach

An **adapter** is a small set of additional weights inserted into the transformer layers.  
Only these adapters are trained during fine‑tuning; the original model weights stay unchanged.

### How Adapters Are Placed
A typical transformer block looks like this:

```
Input → Self‑Attention → Adapter₁ → Feed‑Forward → Adapter₂ → Output
```

- **Adapter₁** and **Adapter₂** are the only trainable parts.
- This is repeated for every transformer block in the model.

### Why It Works
Research has shown that by fine‑tuning just **3–4% of the parameters** (the adapters), you can achieve performance nearly identical to full fine‑tuning.

**Analogy:**  
Imagine you have a giant encyclopedia. Instead of rewriting the entire book for each new topic, you insert a small sticky note (adapter) in every chapter that contains the new information. You only rewrite the sticky notes for each topic, keeping the original text intact.

---

## 8. LoRA: Low‑Rank Adaptation (The Next Step)

LoRA is another PEFT method that has become very popular.  
Instead of inserting new layers, LoRA adds trainable low‑rank matrices to existing weights.  
It’s even more memory‑efficient than adapters and is widely used in open‑source models.

*We’ll cover LoRA in detail in the next tutorial – stay tuned!*

## 9. RAG vs. Fine‑Tuning: A Critical Distinction

During the discussion, a common question arose:  
> “Is retrieval‑augmented generation (RAG) a form of fine‑tuning?”

**Short answer:** No.

- **RAG** works by retrieving relevant documents and including them in the prompt at inference time. The model itself is not trained; it simply uses the extra context.
- **Fine‑tuning** changes the model’s weights so it internalises the knowledge.

**When to use which:**
- **RAG** is cheaper and good when you need to reference external documents that change frequently (e.g., company policies).
- **Fine‑tuning** is better when you need the model to deeply learn a domain and you have a fixed set of training examples.

> *You can even combine both: fine‑tune a model on your domain, then use RAG to give it the latest information.*

---

## 10. Practical Insights from Real‑World Use

- **How much data do I need for fine‑tuning?**  
  It depends. With a strong base model, even **50–100 examples** can yield noticeable improvements. Some industry practitioners suggest **50,000 rows** for stable, high‑quality results, but smaller datasets often work well for simple tasks.

- **Can I fine‑tune via APIs like OpenAI?**  
  Yes. OpenAI, Cohere, and others offer fine‑tuning endpoints. You provide your data, they train a custom model for you (the weights stay on their platform).

- **Is my data safe during fine‑tuning?**  
  If you use a local machine or a private cloud, your data stays with you. When using public platforms, check their privacy policies. For sensitive data, self‑hosted solutions are preferred.

- **Can I fine‑tune for a new language?**  
  Yes, but the base model must have seen similar scripts or have a multilingual architecture. Fine‑tuning on even a modest amount of text in a new language can significantly improve performance.

---

## 11. Summary: The Journey from Pre‑training to PEFT

| Stage | Purpose | Data Type | Parameters Trained |
|-------|---------|-----------|-------------------|
| **Pre‑training** | Learn language structure | Unlabelled text | All (once) |
| **Supervised Fine‑Tuning (SFT)** | Follow instructions | Instruction‑response pairs | All (or many) |
| **Full Fine‑Tuning (FFT)** | Domain specialisation | Domain‑specific labelled data | All (or many) |
| **Preference Tuning** | Align with user style | Preference rankings | All (or many) |
| **PEFT (Adapters / LoRA)** | Specialise efficiently | Task data | Small new parameters |
